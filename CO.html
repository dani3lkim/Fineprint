Know the Terms

The words companies use to confuse you — explained in plain English.

Arbitration
A private process where a company-appointed arbitrator (not a judge) settles disputes. By agreeing to arbitration, you typically give up your right to sue in court or join a class action lawsuit.
Class Action Waiver
A clause that stops you from joining a group lawsuit against a company. Instead of suing with thousands of others, you must fight alone — making small claims not worth pursuing.
Indemnification
You agree to cover the company's legal costs and damages if something goes wrong — even if it wasn't your fault. This can be a blank check of liability.
Governing Law
The state or country whose laws apply to any dispute. Companies often pick business-friendly states (like Delaware) so that local consumer protections don't apply to you.
Limitation of Liability
A cap on how much a company owes you if they cause harm. Often limited to what you paid them — meaning a $10/month service caps its liability at $10 even if they lose your entire business.
Force Majeure
"Act of God" clause — lets a party off the hook for not fulfilling the contract due to extraordinary events. Increasingly used broadly by companies to avoid obligations.
Severability
If one part of a contract is found illegal or unenforceable, the rest of the contract still stands. This protects companies when individual clauses get struck down.
Assignment
The company's right to transfer your contract to another company — like after a merger or acquisition — without your consent. Your agreement follows you to the new owner.
EULA
End User License Agreement. The legal contract between you and a software company. You don't own the software — you're buying a license to use it, which can be revoked.
Intellectual Property (IP)
Ownership of creative works. Many ToS agreements claim broad rights to content you create or upload on their platform — always check who actually owns what you post.
Perpetual License
When you upload content, companies often claim a "perpetual, irrevocable license" to use it forever — even if you delete your account or cancel your subscription.
Auto-Renewal
Your subscription automatically renews and charges your card unless you cancel before the renewal date. Cancellation windows are often short and deliberately hard to find.
Non-Disparagement
You agree never to say anything negative about the company publicly — including honest negative reviews. Violating this can expose you to legal action.
Unilateral Modification
The company can change the terms at any time, often with just an email notice or by posting an update on their website. Continuing to use the service means you agreed.
Non-Compete
Restricts you from working for competitors or starting a competing business for a set period. Enforceability varies widely by state — some states like California ban them entirely.
NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)
You agree not to share confidential information. Scope matters — overly broad NDAs can prevent you from discussing your own work experience or even telling people where you work.
EOB (Explanation of Benefits)
A statement from your insurer showing what was billed, what they paid, and what you owe. It is NOT a bill — but it tells you what bill is coming. Review it carefully for errors.
Deductible
The amount you must pay out-of-pocket before your insurance starts covering costs. A $2,000 deductible means you pay the first $2,000 of covered services each year.
Out-of-Pocket Maximum
The most you'll pay in a year before insurance covers 100% of covered costs. Once hit, you pay nothing more for covered services — knowing this number is crucial.
Prior Authorization
Insurance requires advance approval before covering certain procedures or medications. Getting care without it is the most common reason claims get denied.
Balance Billing
When an out-of-network provider bills you for the difference between their charge and what insurance paid. Illegal in many situations under the No Surprises Act (2022).
Upcoding
A billing error (sometimes fraud) where a provider bills for a more expensive service than what was actually performed. One of the most common causes of inflated medical bills.
Coordination of Benefits
When you have two insurance plans, this determines which one pays first. If handled incorrectly, you might be billed for things your secondary insurance should cover.
Medical Necessity
The standard insurers use to decide if a treatment is covered. If they deem something "not medically necessary," they deny the claim — but you can appeal with a doctor's documentation.
In-Network vs. Out-of-Network
In-network providers have contracts with your insurer at lower rates. Out-of-network providers can charge significantly more — always verify network status before a non-emergency procedure.
ADEA Waiver
The Age Discrimination in Employment Act protects workers 40+. To validly waive these rights in a severance agreement, you must be given 21 days to consider it and 7 days to revoke after signing.
Release of All Claims
You permanently give up the right to sue your employer for anything that happened during your employment — discrimination, wage theft, harassment. This is the most consequential clause in any severance agreement.
COBRA
Allows you to continue your employer's health insurance after leaving — but you pay the full premium (employer + employee share). Usually very expensive. You have 60 days to elect coverage after job loss.
Garden Leave
You remain on payroll during your notice period but don't work. Sounds good, but it delays your ability to start a new job and may affect when non-compete clocks start.
Non-Solicitation
Restricts you from poaching former colleagues or clients after leaving. Different from non-compete — you can work in the same industry, you just can't recruit specific people.
Clawback Provision
If you violate any term of the severance agreement (like non-disparagement), the company can demand back all severance payments already made — sometimes with interest.
Unemployment Eligibility
Severance payments can delay or reduce unemployment benefits depending on your state. The structure of your severance (lump sum vs. salary continuation) often matters for eligibility.
Equity Acceleration
Some severance agreements include vesting of unvested stock options or RSUs. Always check if you're leaving unvested equity on the table — this can be worth negotiating.
Security Deposit
Money paid upfront to cover potential damages. Most states cap it (typically 1–2 months rent) and require itemized deductions returned within a specific timeframe — often 14–30 days after move-out.
Implied Warranty of Habitability
A landlord's legal obligation to maintain a livable unit — heat, water, no pests, working locks. This right cannot be waived by a lease clause, even if you sign one that says otherwise.
Joint and Several Liability
If you have roommates, each of you is individually responsible for the entire rent. If a roommate doesn't pay, the landlord can come after you alone for the full amount.
Early Termination Fee
A penalty for breaking your lease early. Legitimate fees typically equal 1–2 months rent. Beware clauses requiring you to pay all remaining rent — some states limit or prohibit these.
Holdover Tenant
If you stay past your lease end date without a new agreement, you become a holdover tenant. Landlords can often charge significantly higher rent — sometimes double — during this period.
Right of Entry
Most states require landlords to give 24–48 hours notice before entering your unit except in emergencies. Lease clauses attempting to waive this requirement may be unenforceable.
Lease Guarantee
A co-signer who agrees to pay if you don't. If a guarantor is required, they take on the same liability as you — including for the full lease term if you default.
Normal Wear and Tear
Deterioration from ordinary use — scuffs on walls, carpet worn from walking. Landlords cannot charge you for this. They can only charge for damage beyond normal use. Document everything on move-in.
FDCPA
Fair Debt Collection Practices Act. A federal law that limits what debt collectors can do — no calling before 8am or after 9pm, no harassment, no false statements. Violations can be sued for $1,000 per violation.
Debt Validation
Within 30 days of first contact, you can demand proof the debt is real and that the collector has the right to collect it. They must stop collection until they provide verification.
Statute of Limitations
The time limit on how long a collector can sue you to collect a debt — typically 3–6 years depending on state and debt type. After this, the debt is "time-barred" and they can't win in court.
Zombie Debt
Old, time-barred debt that collectors buy cheaply and try to resurrect. Making even a small payment can restart the statute of limitations clock, giving them a fresh window to sue you.
Cease and Desist
A written request to stop all contact. Under the FDCPA, collectors must honor it — except to notify you of specific legal actions. Send it certified mail and keep a copy.
Charge-Off
When a creditor writes off the debt as a loss (usually after 180 days of non-payment). The debt still exists and can be sold to collectors — a charge-off doesn't mean you don't owe it.
Debt Buyer
Companies that purchase old debts for pennies on the dollar and then try to collect the full amount. They may have incomplete records and must still prove the debt is valid if challenged.
Credit Reporting Window
Most negative items (collections, charge-offs) can only stay on your credit report for 7 years from the original delinquency date — regardless of when debt was sold or re-aged by a collector.
